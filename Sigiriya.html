History of Sigiriya

Sigiriya, also known as Lion Rock, is an ancient rock fortress built in the 5th century AD by King Kasyapa (477-495 AD). The king chose this dramatic location for his new capital, constructing a palace on the summit and decorating the rock face with colorful frescoes.

The Story of King Kasyapa

King Kasyapa seized the throne after killing his father, King Dhatusena. Fearing retaliation from his brother Moggallana, the rightful heir, Kasyapa built his fortress on the impregnable Sigiriya rock. After 18 years, Moggallana returned with an army, and Kasyapa was defeated in battle. The site was later converted into a Buddhist monastery until the 14th century.

Archaeological Discovery

Sigiriya was largely forgotten until British archaeologist H.C.P. Bell began excavations in the late 19th century. Major restoration work was undertaken in the 1980s, and today it stands as one of Asia's most important archaeological sites.

What to See at Sigiriya

1. Water Gardens

The symmetrical water gardens at the base of the rock are among the oldest landscaped gardens in the world. They feature pools, fountains (still functioning during rainy season), and sophisticated hydraulic systems.

2. Frescoes

The famous Sigiriya frescoes depict celestial maidens ("Sigiriya Damsels") originally thought to number around 500. Only 21 survive today, protected in a sheltered gallery about halfway up the rock.

3. Mirror Wall

This highly polished wall was once so reflective that the king could see himself as he walked by. Over centuries, visitors have inscribed messages on the wall, creating the largest known collection of ancient graffiti.

4. Lion's Paw Entrance

The original entrance was through a massive lion sculpture - only the giant paws remain today. The name "Sigiriya" comes from "Sinhagiri" meaning Lion Rock.

5. Summit Palace

The 1.6 hectare summit contains the ruins of King Kasyapa's palace, including throne platforms, pools, and breathtaking 360-degree views of the surrounding jungle.

6. Boulder Gardens

These gardens integrate natural boulders with man-made structures, including audience halls and monastic retreats built into and around the massive rocks.

Visiting Tips

Best Time to Visit

Time of day: Early morning (7-8 AM) to avoid heat and crowds
Season: December to April (dry season)
Avoid: Afternoon heat and rainy afternoons (May-November)

What to Bring

• Comfortable walking shoes with good grip
• Hat and sunscreen
• Water (1-2 liters per person)
• Camera (no flash for frescoes)
• Small backpack (hands-free for climbing)

Fitness Level

The climb involves approximately 1,200 steps. It's moderately strenuous but manageable for most people with average fitness. Take breaks as needed. Not recommended for those with severe vertigo or heart conditions.

Photography Tips

• Morning light is best for photography
• No flash photography of frescoes
• Wide-angle lens recommended for summit views
• Drone photography requires special permission

Nearby Attractions

Pidurangala Rock

Located just north of Sigiriya, Pidurangala offers an alternative (and cheaper) climb with spectacular views of Sigiriya itself. The climb is slightly easier and takes about 45 minutes.

Dambulla Cave Temple

Just 30 minutes from Sigiriya, this UNESCO site features five caves with over 150 Buddha statues and murals dating back to the 1st century BC.

Minneriya National Park

Famous for "The Gathering" where hundreds of elephants congregate around the reservoir during dry season (July-October). Safari tours available.

Sigiriya Village Tour

Experience rural Sri Lankan life with a bullock cart ride, catamaran trip, and visit to a local village to see traditional crafts and farming.

Sigiriya Frequently Asked Questions

How difficult is the climb?

The climb involves approximately 1,200 steps and takes 1-2 hours up, 1 hour down. It's moderately strenuous but manageable for most people with average fitness. There are railings for support on steep sections.

Is it safe for children and elderly?

Children over 6 usually manage fine with supervision. Elderly visitors should assess their fitness level. The steepest section is the spiral staircase to the frescoes, which can be skipped if needed.

What should I wear?

Comfortable clothing, good walking shoes, hat, and sunscreen. Shoulders and knees don't need to be covered at Sigiriya (unlike temples).

Can I visit if I have a fear of heights?

The climb involves exposure to heights, especially on the metal staircases. If you have severe vertigo, you might find parts challenging. Many people with mild fear of heights manage by focusing on the path ahead.

Is photography allowed?

Yes, except flash photography of the frescoes. Tripods may require special permission. Commercial photography needs prior arrangement.
